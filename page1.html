Why is the internet characterized as "dangerous"?
"Are there really any risks?"
Here are some of the internet's threats...
  1. First problem is cyberbullying.

    Ninety percent of teenagers concur that cyberbullying is an issue, and sixty-three percent think it's a major one. Furthermore, according to a 2018 study on kids' online behavior, 60% of kids who use social media had seen bullying in some capacity, while the majority of kids just chose to ignore the behavior for a variety of reasons. Furthermore, as of February 2018, 47% of all youth reported having experienced cyberbullying, according to enough.org. Today's virtual playground is made up mostly of social media and online games, where a lot of cyberbullying occurs around the clock. In social media conversations, children might be made fun of. Alternatively, in online gaming, their virtual characters may be continuously targeted, transforming the game from a creative journey into a degrading experience that intensifies into cyberbullying on several platforms and in real life. Being at ease discussing with your kids about what's going on in their lives both online and offline (IRL) and how to deal with bullies is the greatest way to guard against cyberbullying. Nothing can replace an open dialogue, however, even while cyber security software and specialized apps for keeping an eye on your child's mobile and internet activity can be helpful.
  2. Second danger is phising.

    Phishing is a prevalent kind of cyberattack that targets people via phone calls, texts, emails, and other correspondence. The goal of a phishing attack is to deceive the target into doing the attacker's intended action, which could involve disclosing sensitive information like login credentials for a system or financial information.Because phishing is so simple, inexpensive, and successful for cybercriminals, it is a serious issue. Phishing techniques are a common cyberattack since they require little money and effort, especially when using email. Phishing scam victims could experience data loss, identity theft, and malware infections, including ransomware. Cybercriminals target sensitive corporate data, like client names, as well as personally identifiable information (PII), such as credit card numbers, bank account information, tax returns, and medical records.and contact details, trade secrets for exclusive products, and private correspondence. Phishing attacks are another tool used by cybercriminals to get direct access to accounts on social media, email, and other platforms, or to get authorization to change and compromise linked systems, such as order processing and point-of-sale terminals. Cybercriminals obtain a modest footing to expand upon from benign phishing emails, which are often the beginning of the largest data breaches.
  3. Third risk is that there is no "delete button".

    The most crucial lesson to impart to kids regarding this is that whatever they publish online will always be there. It has always been there. There is a way for someone to view it even if they later remove it. This might one day make it difficult for them to get employment, be used as blackmail, or cause them to feel harassed or ashamed. Although it can be unsettling for parents to be aware of the threats their children may encounter online, you can play a part in ensuring their safety and teaching them self-defense skills. Address any concerns they might have and outline the main risks associated with using the internet.
  4. Fourth threat is the insecurities created.

    Kids can connect with one another, find new interests, relax, and have fun using apps like TikTok, Snapchat, and Instagram. Yet, being excessively presented to unreasonable pictures of 'magnificence' can once in a while cause kids and youngsters to really regret themselves and their own lives.
